About This Project

Questioning the shape of the world beneath our feet

The Flat Earth Model

The flat earth model proposes that the earth is a plane or disc rather than a spinning sphere hurtling through space. In this model, the North Pole sits at the centre of the disc, while Antarctica forms a massive ice wall around the perimeter, holding in the oceans. The sun and moon are much smaller and closer than mainstream science claims, circling above the plane in patterns that produce day, night, and the seasons.

Proponents argue that our everyday sensory experience supports a flat, motionless earth. We do not feel the alleged 1,040 mph rotation at the equator, nor do we observe the supposed curvature when looking out across large bodies of water. Long-distance photography has captured skylines and landmarks that, according to standard earth curvature calculations, should be hidden well below the horizon.

Gleason's New Standard Map of the World

In 1892, Alexander Gleason published his "New Standard Map of the World," an azimuthal equidistant projection centred on the North Pole. This map is widely used in the flat earth community because it presents all continents arranged around a central point, with Antarctica depicted as a ring of ice around the outer edge — matching the flat earth model's description of the world.

Gleason's map is not merely decorative. The azimuthal equidistant projection preserves true distances from the centre point, making it useful for radio operators, navigators, and the United Nations, whose emblem uses the same projection. The fact that major institutions rely on this projection raises an interesting question: why does the "flat" map work so well for practical distance calculations from a central point?

The Bedford Level Experiment

One of the earliest and most famous experiments testing earth's shape took place along the Old Bedford River in England in 1838. Samuel Birley Rowbotham waded into the river and used a telescope to observe a boat with a flag on its mast as it rowed away along a six-mile stretch of calm water. According to the accepted curvature rate of roughly eight inches per mile squared, the boat should have dropped about 24 feet below the line of sight. Rowbotham reported that the flag remained visible for the entire six miles.

The experiment was repeated multiple times over the following decades with varied results. Critics attributed the visibility to atmospheric refraction — the bending of light through layers of air at different temperatures. Flat earth researchers counter that refraction is invoked inconsistently and that similar observations have been replicated across many different bodies of water around the world.

The Horizon Always Rises to Eye Level

On a globe, as you gain altitude the horizon should increasingly fall below your eye line. Flat earth proponents point out that from commercial aircraft at 35,000 feet, the horizon appears to remain at eye level in every direction, without any need to look downward. Amateur high-altitude balloon footage shows the horizon stretching flat and level across the entire frame when filmed with non-fisheye lenses.

This observation is central to the flat earth argument. If the earth were a sphere with a circumference of roughly 25,000 miles, there should be a noticeable dip at cruising altitude. The persistent flatness of the horizon, they say, is exactly what you would expect on a flat plane extending outward in every direction.

Water Finds Its Level

Water, by its very nature, seeks and finds its own level. It does not curve or cling to the exterior of shapes. Pour water on a basketball and it runs straight off. The oceans, lakes, and rivers of the world lie flat and level, conforming to the flat plane they rest upon. This is one of the most intuitive arguments: the surface of a still lake is not curved — it is level.

Engineers and surveyors routinely assume a flat, non-rotating earth for their calculations. Canal builders, railway engineers, and architects do not factor earth curvature into their designs for projects spanning tens or even hundreds of miles. The Suez Canal, for example, stretches over 100 miles without any allowance for curvature, and the water within it sits perfectly level from end to end.

Perspective and the Vanishing Point

Ships "disappearing" over the horizon bottom-first is often cited as proof of curvature. Flat earth researchers demonstrate that a sufficiently powerful zoom lens or telescope can bring those "disappeared" ships back into full view — hull and all. This suggests that the disappearance is caused by the limits of angular resolution and atmospheric perspective, not curvature.

The laws of perspective state that parallel lines converge to a vanishing point. On a flat plane, objects recede toward this vanishing point until they become too small to resolve. The sun setting is explained the same way — it moves away and shrinks to a point on the horizon rather than physically dropping below a curve. This perspective effect, combined with atmospheric conditions, creates the illusion of a curved surface.

Questioning the Narrative

Regardless of where you stand on the shape of the earth, the flat earth movement represents something important: a willingness to question authority and verify claims through personal observation and independent research. Throughout history, accepted scientific models have been overturned — the geocentric model, spontaneous generation, luminiferous aether — and the pursuit of truth requires that every model remain open to scrutiny.

This project exists to present the flat earth perspective and let visitors explore the Gleason map interactively. We encourage everyone to do their own research, conduct their own experiments, and draw their own conclusions. The truth does not fear investigation.
